Surgetica 2019

Nervous System Exploration Using Tractography To Enhance Pelvic Surgery

Pelvic surgery raises the challenge of preservation of nerves that handle urinary, genital and digestive functions, especially in situations where these structures may be modified by tumors or malformations. Recent works on 3D nerve visualization, that rely on cadavers dissections or intra-operative use of probes detecting myelin-binding fluorophores, do not provide pre- or post-operative analysis of the pelvic nervous anatomy. Magnetic resonance neurography as in requires a slice by slice manual segmentation of the nerves. Diffusion MRI, associated with tractography algorithms, is current with no need for manual nerve segmentation. In contrast to brain imaging that motivated a lot of work, only few studies focus on peripheral nerves visualization. In this paper, we propose a method for pelvic tractography analysis based on patient-specific organ segmentation. Results on an healthy adult subject constitute a first proof of concept.

Ultrasound in Obstetrics and Gynecology

Biometric and Morphological Features of the Fetal Bladder in Lower Urinary Tract Obstruction on Magnetic Resonance Imaging. New Perspectives for Fetal Cystoscopy.

Since the first description of fetal cystoscopy (FC) by Quintero et al in 1995, many attempts have been made to relieve the outlet blockage in fetal lower urinary tract obstruction (LUTO) caused by posterior urethral valves (PUV). Despite overly optimistic reports on the matter, several authors agree that the anatomy of a distended bladder and inadequate instrumentation account for most technical limitations of FC. The development of an appropriate fetal cystoscope, that would answer anatomical constraints, is therefore justified. However such an anatomical study has yet to be published. The main challenge when performing a FC is getting around the vesicourethral angle to enter the posterior urethra6, which is thought to vary according to GA, but also probably with bladder volume. In order to support this theory, we carried out an in vivo MRI-based anatomical study of the fetal bladder in cases with LUTO and compared its biometric features to those of non-uropathic fetal bladder.

ISBI 2019

White Matter Multi-Resolution Segmentation Using Fuzzy Set Theory

The neural architecture of the white matter of the brain, obtained using tractography algorithms, can be divided into different tracts. Their function might be affected in some syndromes or conditions and is in many cases still an object of study. Obtaining a reproducible and correct segmentation is therefore crucial both in clinics and in research. However, it is difficult to obtain due to the huge number of fibers and high inter-subject variability. In this paper, we propose to segment and recognize tracts by using anatomical definitions, which are usually based on relationships between structures. Since these definitions are mainly qualitative, we propose to model their intrinsic vagueness using fuzzy spatial relations and combine them into a single quantitative score mapped to each fiber. To cope with the high redundancy of tractograms and ease interpretation, we also take advantage of a simplification scheme based on a multi-resolution representation. This allows for an interactive and real-time navigation through different levels of detail. We illustrate our method using the Human Connectome Project dataset and compare it to other well-known white matter segmentation techniques.

OHBM 2018

Segmentation of White Matter Tractograms Using Fuzzy Spatial Relations

Clinicians or researchers often want to isolate precise white matter tracts in order to test hypotheses related to a pathology. Segmenting white matter into reproducible tracts is difficult due to the huge amount of fibers and their vague anatomical definitions. The most common method, selecting fibers passing through manually delineated ROIs, is time-consuming and poorly reproducible for tracts with convoluted trajectories. Differently, manually segmented ROIs can be transferred from training images to test subjects via non-linear deformations. The resulting segmentation might not be accurate when training and test images do not share the same topology (e.g. due to a tumor). Other methods rely on unsupervised clustering and use a labeled atlas. With a different perspective, a near-to-English query language (WMQL) allows the user to interactively define divisional terms, relative clauses and logical operations to segment anatomical tracts. However, the shape of the resulting tracts can vary among subjects since WMQL is based on simple binary relations and bounding boxes. We propose to include qualitative anatomical definitions, modeled as fuzzy sets, into a semi-automatic segmentation algorithm, and to automatically compute an “anatomical coherence score” for every fiber of the tractogram.
